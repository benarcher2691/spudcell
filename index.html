Status Briefing · 4 July 2026

SpudCell — Development Status

The first bottom-up synthetic cell shown to run a complete cell cycle — feeding, growth, genome replication, and division — built entirely from chemically defined, non-living components.

Kate Adamala Lab · University of Minnesota Twin Cities · Preprint released 1 July 2026

▶ View the slide deck 📄 Primary manuscript (PDF) ⤓ Report (Markdown)

TL;DR

SpudCell is the closest anyone has come to building a living cell from scratch — a fatty droplet holding a defined ~90 kb genome (~36 genes) that can feed, grow, copy its genome, and divide. But it is not alive and not yet peer-reviewed: it can't make its own ribosomes, regulate metabolism, or clear waste, and it was released as a preprint after a rejection from Cell. Reactions range from "stunning breakthrough" to firm caution.

1 · What SpudCell is

  • A synthetic minimal cell: liposomes (~µm fatty droplets) holding a defined genome plus a cell-free protein-expression system.
  • Built bottom-up — every component known — rather than by stripping down a natural organism (the top-down approach Craig Venter used in 2010).
  • Genome: ~90 kbp across seven plasmids, roughly 50× smaller than a bacterium; news coverage summarizes this as ~36 genes, from E. coli and viral sources (notably the Phi29 replicase).
  • Runs on the PURE system (Protein synthesis Using Recombinant Elements) encapsulated inside the liposomes.
  • Name: coined to evoke Sputnik; Adamala also jokes, "I'm Polish… I'm mostly made of potatoes."

2 · Who is behind it

  • Lead: Dr. Katarzyna (Kate) P. Adamala, University of Minnesota Twin Cities.
  • Manuscript authors: Gaut, Deich, Cash, Hoog, Engelhart, Adamala — a 190-page manuscript representing ~5 researcher-years.
  • Biotic: a new public-benefit research institute co-founded by Adamala, Stanford's Drew Endy, and two others. Seed funding ~$10M; first grants expected September 2026. Endy's framing: build "an operating system for life."

3 · What has been demonstrated

FunctionMechanism
Feeding / growthGenetically encoded fusion: modified α-hemolysin displays a His-tag; Ni-NTA "feeder" vesicles bind and fuse, delivering lipids, enzymes, ribosomesshown
Genome replicationPhi29 replicase; rolling-circle amplification (TTcDR)shown
DivisionFLAG tag + streptavidin repulsion splits the droplet; also mechanical extrusionshown
SelectionA growth-boosting mutation outcompeted originals (~60% of genomes after 5 rounds)shown

The long-standing hurdle it clears is coupling growth and division to gene expression — hard because each function tends to need different conditions (magnesium, pH).

4 · What it cannot do

  • Cannot make its own ribosomes — they degrade with no replacement
  • Cannot regulate metabolism or clear waste
  • Wholly dependent on a nutrient-rich medium (ATP) and feeder vesicles
  • Division inefficient — only ~30% keep the full genome after 5 cycles
  • "Conks out" after a few generations
  • Not true Darwinian evolution — the mutation was inserted by hand; no cytoskeleton

Adamala's framing: "It is not as robust, as fast, or as good at most of its functions as a natural cell, but it is proof of principle."

5 · Publication status & controversy

  • Not yet peer-reviewed — released as a preprint for open scrutiny.
  • The manuscript was rejected by Cell, where one reviewer argued SpudCells "were not real biology."
  • Adamala then sent the 190-page manuscript to journalists under embargo, in some cases ahead of bioRxiv — an unusual sequence that drew criticism. The group says it will submit to a new journal soon.
  • Her rationale: "This is like the spark" — releasing fast to jump-start a coordinated field.

6 · How the field is reacting

Supportive

  • Roseanna Zia (Missouri): "a stunning scientific achievement."
  • Job Boekhoven (TU Munich): "the feat the field has been waiting for."
  • Drew Endy (Stanford): "a catalyzing moment."
  • Tom Ellis (Imperial): "biggest breakthrough in recent times."

Cautious / critical

  • Seraphine Wegner (Münster): "cool, but not close to a fully synthetic cell."
  • Kerstin Göpfrich (Heidelberg): critical of the publicity-first rollout.
  • John Dupré (Exeter): questions the practical point and the missing "relational" side of life.

7 · Significance & where it's headed

Why it matters: the first fully defined, non-living-origin cell to couple growth and division to gene expression — offering both a fully understood chassis for engineering biology (drugs, foods, fuels, materials) and a window into the minimum requirements for life.

Next steps (per the manuscript & Biotic): more robust, higher-yield, controllable division; a route toward a synthetic cytoskeleton; coupling growth + division + spontaneous mutation into genuine Darwinian evolution; and metabolic self-sufficiency (self-made ribosomes).
